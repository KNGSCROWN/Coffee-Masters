A Brief History of Coffee: The Almighty Bean

From Ethiopia's highlands to your neighborhood café — centuries of travel, smuggling, and culture

The Kaldi Legend (c. A.D. 800–900): A young Ethiopian goatherd named Kaldi noticed his goats became unusually lively after eating red berries from a shrub. A passing monk tried the berries as tea — and discovered it kept him alert during evening prayers. This is the most enduring origin story of coffee.
c. 800–900 AD · Ethiopia
Discovery & Early Use
Ethiopians chew coffee beans and leaves, brew them as tea with boiling water, or grind unroasted beans with animal fat. Coffee becomes part of Ethiopian culture.
~1000–1400 · Yemen & Arabia
First Roasting & Trade Begins
Beans cross the Red Sea to Yemen. For the first time, they are roasted and boiled to make qahwa (literally "that which prevents sleep"). Yemen becomes the world's first large-scale coffee farming region. The port of Mocha becomes the dominant export hub for Coffea arabica.
1536 · Ottoman Empire
Turks Control the Trade — & the Beans
The Ottoman Turks occupy Yemen and turn coffee into a major export. To maintain monopoly, they require all beans leaving Yemen to be steeped, roasted, or baked first — preventing germination and cultivation elsewhere.
1555 · Constantinople
The World's First Coffeehouses
Called kaveh kanes, these coffeehouses open in Constantinople (Istanbul). They become hubs for music, chess, conversation and news — the original "third place." Under Turkish law, a man's failure to provide coffee was grounds for divorce.
1600s · Global Smuggling
Seeds Smuggled Out of Yemen
Muslim pilgrim Baba Budan straps 7 coffee seeds around his waist and cultivates them in India. The Dutch smuggle a coffee tree to Holland, then to Java, Sumatra, and Ceylon (Sri Lanka), beginning global spread.
1645–1700 · Europe
Coffeehouse Culture Sweeps Europe
Venice's first café opens in 1645. London's "penny universities" (1652) serve coffee with newspapers. Paris's Café Procope (1686) becomes Europe's first literary coffeehouse — still open today. Vienna develops the style of straining grounds and adding cream.
1714–1730 · Americas
Coffee Reaches the New World
Louis XIV receives the "Noble Tree" as a gift. French naval officer Gabriel de Clieu steals a shoot and plants it in Martinique, from which coffee spreads to Jamaica, Cuba, and Central America. Francisco de Melo Palheta secretly obtains seeds via a romantic affair — the start of Brazilian coffee, now the world's largest producer.
1773 · North America
Boston Tea Party → Coffee Nation
After the Tea Act, Americans see drinking coffee as a patriotic act. Coffee overtakes tea as the daily hot beverage of choice in the U.S.
1966 · Berkeley, CA
Alfred Peet & Specialty Coffee
Alfred Peet opens Peet's Coffee and Tea, introducing dark-roasted European-style specialty coffee to America. He becomes the mentor of Starbucks' three founders.
1971 · Seattle, WA
First Starbucks Opens
Jerry Baldwin, Zev Siegl, and Gordon Bowker open Starbucks at Pike Place Market — selling freshly roasted beans only, no brewed coffee yet.
1982–1987 · Seattle
Howard Schultz & the Italian Coffeehouse Vision
Schultz visits Milan, envisions bringing espresso bar culture to the U.S. He opens Il Giornale in 1986, then buys Starbucks in 1987 with 6 stores. By 1992: 165 stores in 5 markets, publicly traded.

The Coffee Tree: Bearer of the Bean

Species, varietals, growth stages, and the year in the life of a coffee tree

Coffea arabica

~65% of world production. Grown at high altitudes. Refined flavor, body, and acidity. More susceptible to disease and pests. Starbucks purchases only arabica. Self-pollinating — most descended from a small pool of Ethiopian seeds.

Coffea canephora (Robusta)

Hardy, disease-resistant, grown at lower elevations. Higher caffeine. Higher yield but less refined, sometimes harsh flavor. Sells at lower price — used as a filler blend. Starbucks does not purchase robusta.

From Seed to Mature Tree: 5 Stages

1
Rising from the Soil (0–6 weeks)
Bean is placed in fertile soil. Roots begin spreading; a green stem emerges. Called the "soldier/matchstick" stage — tiny seedling resembles a helmeted soldier.
2
Butterfly Stage (2 months)
Parchment shell falls away, revealing first true leaves shaped like butterfly wings. The nursery protects the plant from frost, drought, and weeds.
3
Health Checkup (4 months)
First branches develop. Farmers evaluate size, structure, leaf color, branch distance, and root system. Any unhealthy plant is discarded — quality starts here.
4
Ready for the Fields (~1 year)
Dark green color, healthy foliage, prominent roots. The young tree is transplanted to its permanent home in the coffee field. Now the farmer waits.
5
Mature Tree (3–5 years)
Flowers every year, producing cherries. Takes ~9 months from flowering to ripe cherries. A healthy tree can produce for 25–30 years. Pruned to 5–6 ft for easy harvest.
Hacienda Alsacia: Starbucks' own 240-hectare research farm on the slopes of Poás Volcano, Costa Rica (purchased 2013). Home to 350 different varietals across 25 dedicated research acres, and the Core Collection — 100 of the most genetically diverse arabica strains in the world, sourced 50+ years ago from Ethiopia. Research focus: breeding disease-resistant, climate-resilient arabica hybrids.

The Coffee Cherry: Influencer of the Bean

Harvest, anatomy, and how processing methods shape the flavors in your cup

Anatomy of the Coffee Cherry — 6 Layers

1. Outer Skin

Taut, thick, bitter outermost layer. When peeled, the cherry looks like a peeled grape or lychee.

2. Pulp

Mostly sugar and water. Makes up ~80% of the cherry's total weight — a lot of waste. Farms recycle it as compost.

3. Mucilage

Sticky, honey-like coating under the pulp. Its contact time with the bean is the #1 driver of flavor differences between processing methods.

4. Parchment

Thin, papery protective layer — similar to the skin on a peanut.

5. Silver Skin

Almost translucent final layer. Flakes off as "chaff" during roasting.

6. Green Bean (the seed)

The actual coffee bean — usually 2 flat-sided beans, face to face. In 5–10% of cherries, one round "peaberry" develops instead.

Processing Methods — How They Shape Flavor

Most common worldwide Requires clean water Crisp, clean, bright

Mucilage is quickly removed — so it has the least influence on cup flavor. Results in pleasant acidity, nuanced flavors, and a clean finish.

1
Receiving
Cherries arrive at a wet mill and go into a receiving tank.
2
De-pulping
Machine slices into fruit, beans pop out. Skin and pulp saved for compost.
3
Removing Mucilage
Traditional: fermentation tank for 18–36 hours (natural enzymes break down mucilage). Modern: demucilaging machine using <5% of the water.
4
Drying (3–7 days)
Parchment-covered beans laid on patios or raised drying tables. Raked and turned all day for even drying.
5
Resting (up to 2 months)
Bagged parchment coffee rests in a warehouse, continuing to develop flavor.
6
Hulling
Hulling machines remove parchment. Green beans are in their final state — ready for export and roasting.
Indonesia only (giling basah) Earthy, herbal, syrupy body Very low acidity

Unique to smallholder farmers on Indonesian islands. High humidity made traditional washed processing impractical. Beans are hulled while still semi-wet — which gives Indonesian coffees their distinctive full body and earthy/herbal character.

Key difference: De-pulped on the farm using a hand-cranked luwak. Mucilage is only roughly removed (some stays for flavor). Hulled in a semi-wet state — unlike any other method. Finishes drying as a green bean (not in parchment).

Oldest method East Africa & Brazil Intense fruit, full body

No water is used. The entire fruit dries around the bean. Sugars from pulp and skin infuse into the bean, creating fruity, wine-like flavors. Africa: fresh fruit (cherry, blueberry). Latin America: dried fruit (raisin, plum, fig).

Key steps: Cherries spread in thin layers on patios or raised beds → turned regularly so outer fruit doesn't rot → after several days, skin becomes hard like raisins → hulled to remove all layers together → final resting.

Costa Rica & Central America Sweet, creamy, muted acidity

A hybrid of washed and natural. Skips fermentation. Mucilage clings to parchment and dries with the bean. Classified by color (Yellow → Black) based on the amount of mucilage left and drying time. No actual honey involved — the name comes from the sticky, sugary nature of mucilage itself.

Yellow honey: less mucilage, faster drying. Black honey: most mucilage, longest drying = most intense sweetness.

Most common in Brazil Sweet, more body than washed

Shares traits of washed and natural. Cherries are de-pulped and partially demucilaged — some mucilage intentionally left to dry on the parchment. No fermentation tank. Results in a flavorful, sweet cup with more body and less acidity than washed coffees.

Coffee-Growing Countries: A Closer Look at Origin

The Coffee Belt spans 3 regions, 70+ countries, and millions of farms

The Coffee Belt: The tropical and sub-tropical zone between the Tropics of Cancer and Capricorn. Coffee grows best here thanks to warm temperatures, rainfall, and altitude. Three main regions: Latin America, Africa, and Asia/Pacific. Starbucks sources from 450,000+ farms, 90%+ of which are smallholders.

Terroir = Soil + Altitude + Microclimate

The unique combination of these three factors is why two coffees grown even on the same farm can taste completely different. Volcanic soil (rich in minerals) → complex sugars and organic acids. Higher altitude → slower cherry growth → denser beans → more complex flavor. Starbucks buys arabica from 3,000–6,000 ft elevation.

Farm Types

Estates: 50+ hectares, process on-site. Micro-estates: smaller, own micro-mills. Smallholder farms: 2–5 hectares — where most Starbucks coffee comes from. Cooperatives: groups of 100–15,000 smallholder farms sharing a mill and profits. 1 hectare ≈ 1.3 soccer fields.

Nuts, cocoa, soft spiceChocolate, toffeeBaking spices, jasmine, citrus

By volume, Latin America produces most of the world's specialty coffee. The region features the Sierra Madre and Andes mountain ranges — rich volcanic soil, high elevations, diverse microclimates. Home to many Starbucks Farmer Support Centers.

Colombia

Multiple microclimates along the Andes. Two main growing regions (Antioquia, Cauca) produce very different cups from the same Caturra varietal — showing terroir at work. Has 2 harvests/year (fly-crop).

Brazil

World's #1 arabica producer. Large estates, heavily mechanized. Leader in pulped natural processing. 2015's Cup of Excellence winner scored a record-breaking 95.18 points.

Guatemala

14 distinct ecosystems. Active volcanoes provide mineral-rich soil. Known for 23+ active volcanoes. As of 2019, Starbucks has donated 30M+ disease-resilient trees in Mexico, Guatemala, and El Salvador.

Costa Rica

Small but hugely influential. Government-committed to coffee quality. Home to Hacienda Alsacia — Starbucks' own farm and R&D center. Pioneering honey processing methods.

Honduras

Largest Central American producer. 15 of 18 departments grow coffee. Challenge: rainy climate makes post-harvest drying very difficult.

El Salvador

"Land of Volcanoes" — 23 active. Has retained many Bourbon trees and the larger-bean Pacamara varietal. Pacamara originated at Montecarlos Estate here.

Peru

Coffee mainly in Andes at 4,000+ ft. Mostly Typica varietal — which was devastated by the 2013–14 coffee leaf rust infestation affecting 50%+ of plantations. Recovering steadily.

Citrus, berry, spiceFloral aromasJammy fruit, sparkling acidity

Birthplace of coffee. ~5 million smallholder farmers. Known for the most distinctive and celebrated flavor profiles in the world. Starbucks operates FSCs in Rwanda (2009), Tanzania (2011), and Ethiopia (2014).

Ethiopia

The legendary birthplace of coffee. ~50% of its production is consumed domestically — daily coffee ceremonies are central to social life. Thousands of heirloom varietals. Arabica grows wild in Southwestern Ethiopia.

Kenya

Known for fully washed coffees with intense, juicy flavors — black currant, fresh berries, tart grapefruit. Rich volcanic soil around Mt. Kenya. Cooperatives of smallholder farms. 2 harvests/year (fly-crop = mitaca).

Rwanda

"Land of a Thousand Hills." Coffee played a major role in rebuilding after the 1994 genocide. Women of Rwanda — notably the Hingakawa group of co-op Abakundakawa — helped restore the coffee industry. Notes of sweet citrus, honey, dark chocolate.

D.R. Congo

Some of world's most fertile land. Heirloom arabica trees on Lake Kivu slopes. Conflict disrupted the industry for decades. Starbucks' first D.R. Congo single-origin offering came in 2015 — from 4,000+ farmers exporting for the very first time.

Herbs, earth, full bodySyrupy, muted acidityMalty sweetness, butterscotch

Home to some of the world's most distinctive coffees. Most beans here are semi-washed (wet-hulled). Notable for very different flavor profiles than Africa or Latin America. Starbucks FSCs in China (2012) and Sumatra (2016).

Indonesia (Sumatra)

Thousands of volcanic islands. Wet-hulled (giling basah) processing is unique here — creates the earthy, full-bodied, herbal Sumatra profile. Sumatra has been a Starbucks staple since 1971. Women play the leading role in coffee production.

China — Yunnan Province

Coffee growing since late 1800s. Farms range from small to 2,000+ hectares. C.A.F.E. Practices used to elevate quality. Starbucks FSC has trained ~20,000 farmers. Starbucks' "South of the Clouds Blend" was first to include Yunnan beans (2009).

Papua New Guinea

Washed arabica grown in highlands in small "coffee gardens" — sometimes just a few dozen trees per plot. Extremely rural (80%+ non-urban). Remote roads make transport very challenging. Highly distinctive quality.

Ethical Sourcing: Where Our Heart Is

C.A.F.E. Practices, Farmer Support Centers, and Starbucks' commitment to the supply chain

Ethical Sourcing means purchasing coffee in a responsible, sustainable way — ensuring safe, fair working conditions for farmers, respecting environmental impacts, and investing in the long-term viability of coffee-growing communities. Starbucks has held this commitment since 1971.

C.A.F.E. Practices

Coffee and Farmer Equity Practices. Created in 1998 in partnership with Conservation International. One of the coffee industry's first sustainability standards verified by SCS Global Services (third-party experts). A comprehensive framework — not just a label, but continuous improvement.

1. Quality (Prerequisite)

  • Every coffee must meet rigorous arabica taste standards before purchase
  • Starbucks Coffee Trading Co. (SCTC) in Lausanne, Switzerland cups every offer sample
  • Higher quality = higher price paid to farmer
  • Evaluated on: aroma, acidity, body, flavor, complexity + physical bean analysis

2. Economic Transparency (Prerequisite)

  • Suppliers must prove how much of the green coffee price actually reaches the farmer
  • Payment receipts required: quantity, price, buyer, date
  • Ensures farmers get the prices needed to sustain their businesses
  • Starbucks pays premiums above commercial market price

3. Social Responsibility (Verified)

  • 70+ indicators covering workers' rights and conditions
  • Zero-tolerance: no child labor, no forced labor, minimum wage required
  • Covers: wages, freedom of association, hours of work, housing, access to education and medical care
  • Zero-tolerance violations = farm removed until corrected

4. Environmental Leadership (Verified)

  • 100+ indicators covering conservation and sustainable farming
  • Zero-tolerance: no conversion of natural forest to farmland; no WHO-banned pesticides
  • Covers: water conservation, biodiversity, shade canopy, erosion control, climate change adaptation
  • Program regularly reviewed and updated based on climate change and farmer feedback

Farmer Support Centers (FSCs)

Starbucks offices that house agronomists and quality experts. They provide free, open-source technical support to farmers — whether or not those farmers sell to Starbucks. Research from Hacienda Alsacia is shared freely with the entire coffee industry.

9 FSC locations: Costa Rica (2004) · Guatemala (2005) · Rwanda (2009) · Tanzania (2011) · Colombia (2012) · China-Yunnan (2012) · Ethiopia (2014) · Sumatra (2016) · Mexico (2016)

Global Farmer Fund

$50 million commitment to provide financing to coffee farmers. Farmers in rural areas often can't access credit — too large for microloans, too small for conventional loans. The Fund offers loans even to farmers who don't sell to Starbucks.

100 Million Coffee Trees

Began in 2015 as "One Tree for Every Bag." Combating coffee leaf rust in Latin America by donating healthy, rust-resistant trees. Goal: 100 million trees by 2025. As of writing, ~40 million donated. As of 2019, first Guatemala trees produced their first harvest.

The Starbucks Foundation

Awarded $20M+ in Origin Grants to organizations in coffee communities. Focus: vocational training, clean water, health services, and — critically — leadership opportunities for women and families in coffee-growing regions.

Coffee Buyers (SCTC)

Just 7 buyers purchase all of Starbucks' coffee from Lausanne, Switzerland. They buy transparently — price based on quality, offering price stability. Contracts are often finalized years in advance to reduce volatility for farmers.
